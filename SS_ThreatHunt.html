Threat Hunt Report:

Business Email Compromise Investigation  |  Analyst: Matt McClintock  |  4/15/26

Trigger

LogN Pacific Financial Services received an urgent call from their bank's fraud department. A vendor payment of £24,500 was redirected to an unknown account. The receiving bank flagged the transaction as suspicious and froze the funds. The finance team insists they followed standard procedures.

Initial Report: Finance employee Mark Smith reported "weird MFA notifications" on the evening of 25 February. He was at home watching TV and kept getting prompted to approve a sign-in. He assumed it was an IT glitch and eventually approved one to make them stop. The next morning, colleagues discovered inbox rules nobody created.

Objective: Confirm the compromise. Identify the attacker's infrastructure. Scope the damage. Determine what inbox rules were created, what emails were sent, and what data was accessed. Identify the threat actor.

Investigation Summary

Summary

On the evening of 25 February 2026, threat actor Scattered Spider compromised the Microsoft 365 account of finance employee Mark Smith at LogN Pacific Financial Services via an MFA fatigue attack — repeatedly pushing authentication requests until Mark approved one to stop the prompts. The attacker's session originated from the Netherlands and bypassed Conditional Access controls entirely.

Once inside, the attacker created two malicious inbox rules: one to silently forward financial emails to an external address, and another to automatically delete incoming security alerts. Using Mark's compromised account, they sent a fraudulent email to a finance colleague via thread hijacking, redirecting a vendor payment of £24,500 to a fraudulent account. The receiving bank flagged the transaction and froze the funds. The attacker also accessed Mark's OneDrive and SharePoint during the same session, indicating potential data exposure.

Immediate containment required disabling Mark's account and forcing a password reset. Longer-term recommendations include phishing-resistant MFA and Conditional Access enforcement.

Lessons Learned

Control Failure — MFA Push Notifications Standard MFA push notifications are vulnerable to fatigue attacks. The lack of number-matching or additional context in the push prompt meant Mark had no way to distinguish a legitimate sign-in from an attacker's prompt, making approval under duress likely.
Control Failure — No Conditional Access Policy The attacker signed in from an unrecognised IP in the Netherlands with no Conditional Access policy in place to challenge or block the attempt. A policy enforcing compliant device or trusted location requirements would have prevented access entirely.
Control Failure — No Alerting on Inbox Rule Creation The attacker created two malicious inbox rules without triggering any alert. Automated detection of inbox rule creation events — particularly those involving external forwarding addresses — would have surfaced the compromise hours earlier.
Process Gap — Payment Verification The finance team processed a payment based solely on an email instruction, without a secondary verification step such as a phone call to the requestor. A dual-verification procedure for payment detail changes would have stopped the fraudulent transfer regardless of the email compromise.

Recommendations

Immediate

  • Disable Mark Smith's Microsoft 365 account and revoke all active sessions.
  • Force a password reset upon account restoration.
  • Remove all attacker-created inbox rules from Mark Smith's mailbox.
  • Coordinate with the receiving bank to recover the frozen £24,500 transfer.
  • Conduct a full review of files accessed in OneDrive and SharePoint during the attacker's session.

Short-Term

  • Enable number-matching and additional context in MFA push notifications to mitigate fatigue attacks.
  • Implement Conditional Access policies requiring compliant or hybrid-joined devices and blocking sign-ins from high-risk geographies.
  • Enable Microsoft Defender for Cloud Apps anomaly detection, specifically for inbox rule creation events involving external forwarding addresses.

Long-Term

  • Deploy FIDO2 phishing-resistant authentication (hardware security keys) for all finance and privileged accounts.
  • Establish a dual-verification process for any payment detail change requests — email alone should never be sufficient authorisation.
  • Implement DMARC, DKIM, and SPF on all company domains to reduce spoofing risk.
  • Provide targeted security awareness training for finance staff on BEC and thread hijacking indicators.
  • Establish a dedicated out-of-band alert channel for MFA anomalies that bypasses email-based notifications.
